Context

Netflix is the largest subscription video-on-demand (SVOD) service, delivering movies and series over its own Open Connect CDN. It began in 1997 as DVD-by-mail (Reed Hastings and Marc Randolph, Scotts Valley CA), launched streaming in 2007, and now runs on subscription tiers plus a fast-growing ad-supported plan. For an index builder the object is not a page but the title — a movie or series keyed by a numeric title ID at /title/{id}. The valuable corpus (feature video, dubbed audio, timed-text subtitles) sits behind DRM with no legitimate bulk path, but Netflix voluntarily publishes two things an index can use: rich schema.org metadata on public title pages, and two bulk engagement datasets (a weekly Top 10 TSV and a semiannual "What We Watched" report). The notable delta from most large platforms: Netflix's robots.txt grants named AI crawlers the same access as Googlebot. inferred Netflix treats its public metadata as an SEO/AI asset, not something to wall off. Basis: robots.txt lists GPTBot, ClaudeBot, PerplexityBot and OAI-SearchBot in the same Allow: / group as the search engines (Probe Log).

Retrieval Notes (site-specific deltas)

Only where Netflix differs from the default platform assumptions.

Identifier & dedup

The numeric title ID in /title/{id} is the stable key. Each title renders at ~599 locale paths (/fr-en/title/{id}, /sn/title/{id}, …) all hreflang-alternating to the same ID (Probe Log). Dedup on title ID, never on URL.

Retrieval unit

The title (movie or series). Series expose numberOfSeasons but seasons and episodes are not separate public pages; episode granularity appears only inside engagement data (season_title in the Top 10 TSV).

Cross-modal joins

Title ID joins metadata ↔ key art ↔ trailer cleanly. But the engagement datasets key on human-readable show_title/season_title strings, not the numeric ID — so binding engagement to the catalog needs fuzzy title matching, and the match-failure rate is a real quality metric.

Authoritative content field

JSON-LD is the cleanest machine field. The netflix.reactContext Falcor blob holds the richest state but needs rendering. OG/<title> are SEO-tuned ("Watch X | Netflix Official Site") and least useful as the canonical name.

Freshness & recrawl

Per-title metadata is near-static; the volatile fact is catalog membership — titles enter and leave on licensing windows. A weekly diff of /sitemap/index detects adds/removes cheaply; engagement refreshes weekly (Top 10) and semiannually (report).

Quality & spam

Not a UGC platform: no user posts, no spam surface. The dominant defect is staleness — a removed title can leave a cached page and JSON-LD claiming availability that no longer exists in a given country.

Internal vs external search

/search is robots-disallowed; discovery inside Netflix is personalized recommendation, not query. External indexes only ever see the public title pages, so "is X on Netflix" queries route to those pages, not to Netflix's own search.

User query profile

Availability and lookup intent: "is X on Netflix", cast, season count, "new on Netflix". Answerable from title-page JSON-LD; the personalized browse experience is not indexable.

Indexing Plan

  • Retrieval unit: the title (numeric ID); key art, trailer, cast, genre, and engagement stats attach to it.
  • Primary keys & joins: Netflix title ID as the spine; join engagement data via a title-string concordance (lossy — track match rate).
  • Ingestion paths, ranked: 1) /sitemap/index → 25.7K title IDs → crawl one canonical locale per title for JSON-LD (robots-allowed, incl. AI agents). 2) Top 10 global TSV weekly (no auth). 3) "What We Watched" XLSX semiannually for long-tail engagement. 4) Feature video / audio / subtitles — no legitimate path (DRM); design around metadata + trailers.
  • Recrawl cadence: title metadata monthly; catalog-membership diff of the sitemap weekly; Top 10 weekly (Tuesdays); engagement report semiannual.
  • Derivatives to generate: a title-ID ↔ title-string concordance to bind engagement to catalog; synopsis + key-art embeddings; ASR on trailers (the only legally fetchable video).
  • Demo to build: a sitemap-diff catalog tracker — pull /sitemap/index weekly, diff title IDs to flag adds/removes, enrich each with JSON-LD, attach Top-10 engagement by title-string match, and surface the match-failure rate as the headline metric.

Risks to the Index

  • The valuable modalities have no legitimate bulk path: feature video, dubbed audio, and subtitles are DRM-locked on Open Connect. Plan for metadata + trailers permanently.
  • Engagement data is voluntary and unkeyed: the Top 10 TSV and the semiannual report are published at Netflix's discretion and key on title strings, not IDs; format changes or discontinuation would break the join.
  • inferred The AI-crawler allowance is a revocable policy, not a right. Basis: robots.txt currently grants GPTBot, ClaudeBot, PerplexityBot and OAI-SearchBot the Googlebot allow-list (observed 2026-07-01); that grouping can be removed at any time.
  • Geo-fragmentation: the catalog differs by country and the sitemap enumerates ~599 locale variants per title; a single-locale crawl under-samples per-country availability.

Inferences

  • Netflix opens its public catalog metadata to AI crawlers rather than walling it off. Basis: robots.txt lists GPTBot, ClaudeBot, PerplexityBot, OAI-SearchBot, Google-Extended and Claude-User/Claude-SearchBot in the same Allow: / group as the search engines (observed 2026-07-01).
  • Engagement data cannot be cleanly joined to the catalog. Basis: the Top 10 TSV and the engagement report key on show_title/season_title strings, while title pages key on the numeric title ID; neither dataset carries the other's key.
  • The only legally fetchable video is the trailer. Basis: title-page JSON-LD exposes a trailer.contentUrl (.m3u8) while feature streams sit behind DRM on nflxvideo.net with no public URL.
