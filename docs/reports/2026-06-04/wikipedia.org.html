Context

Wikipedia is the volunteer-written encyclopedia operated by the Wikimedia Foundation, a US 501(c)(3) funded almost entirely by donations ($189.5M of $208.6M FY2024-25 revenue — Verified Metrics). It inverts every default platform assumption this methodology carries: no ads, no personalization, no API paywall, no AI-crawler bans, full database dumps, immutable revision IDs, and a no-auth event stream of every edit. The catch is the opposite of the usual one — access is easy, trust is the hard problem, because Wikipedia is a tertiary source whose authority lives in its citations, and because the openness is a policy choice the Foundation is now re-examining as AI scrapers strain its infrastructure (see Risks). For an index builder the real asset is the join structure: every article carries a stable pageid, an immutable revid per version, and a Wikidata QID that links 300+ language editions, Commons media, and external identifiers into one entity graph. inferred Wikipedia is the cheapest large corpus to keep fresh on the web. Basis: the no-auth EventStreams firehose observed in the Probe Log (~27 events/s) plus free full dumps means freshness costs a subscription, not a recrawl budget.

Retrieval Notes (site-specific deltas)

Only where Wikimedia differs from the default platform assumptions — which is almost everywhere.

Identifier & dedup

pageid survives renames; revid is immutable and citable (?oldid= URLs are permanent links). Titles need normalization (underscores/spaces, first-letter case, redirects). The QID is the cross-language key: 300+ editions of one topic share one Wikidata item, so dedup is a solved join, not a similarity problem.

Retrieval unit

Section or infobox field pinned to a specific revid — the article is too long and the lead too lossy. Pinning to revid makes every extracted claim reproducible and diffable, a property almost no other UGC site offers.

Cross-modal joins

Entity-first: article JSON-LD mainEntity, REST summary wikibase_item, Wikidata sitelinks, and Commons depicts (P180) statements all meet at the QID (observed in the Probe Log). Text↔image↔entity alignment with public provenance is the corpus's unique export.

Authoritative content field

No SEO distortion — notability gates entry and external links are nofollow. The real hierarchy: infobox values are Wikidata-or-locally sourced and compact; the lead oversimplifies; the body carries nuance; the citations are the actual authority. For contested claims, Wikipedia is the map to sources, not the source.

Freshness & recrawl

Recrawl is the wrong model. EventStreams pushes every edit with no auth (probe: ~27/s all-wiki); dumps re-baseline twice monthly. Page mutability is extremely skewed: breaking-news pages change by the minute, the long tail sits untouched for years — the stream tells you which is which for free.

Quality & spam

Manipulation is reputational, not commercial spam: paid editing, POV pushing, citation laundering, AI-generated stubs. Machine-readable trust signals exist — protection level, assessment class, maintenance templates, edit-war velocity — and should be indexed as ranking features.

Internal vs external search

On-wiki CirrusSearch is title/redirect-strong but topical-recall-weak; users route broad queries through external engines, which then land on Wikipedia anyway. An index of Wikipedia is partly an index for every other search product.

User query profile

Entity and concept lookups: people, places, events, "what is X". Useless for transactional, local, or live-price intents — Wikipedia content should win orientation queries and yield everything else.

Indexing Plan

  • Retrieval unit: article section pinned to (wiki, pageid, revid); infobox fields and Wikidata statements as structured sub-records.
  • Primary keys & joins: (wiki, pageid) for pages, revid for versions, QID for everything cross-language and cross-modal; redirect table doubles as a query-expansion alias list.
  • Ingestion paths, ranked: 1) XML/JSON dumps — free, complete, 25.3GB bz2 for enwiki text (Probe Log). 2) EventStreams — free, no auth, live deltas. 3) REST/Action APIs — free on-demand hydration under the UA policy. 4) Wikimedia Enterprise — only if daily snapshots and an SLA are worth paying for; the free tier (50K req/mo) covers evaluation.
  • Recrawl cadence: none — event-driven updates from the stream; full dump re-baseline monthly to catch deletions and suppressed revisions.
  • Derivatives to generate: section embeddings, infobox→typed-record extraction, image-caption-entity triples from Commons + depicts, per-page trust features (protection, assessment, churn rate).
  • Demo to build: an EventStreams-driven incremental indexer — subscribe to recentchange, hydrate changed pages via REST summary, and display index staleness in seconds, versus the days a crawler would take.

Risks to the Index

  • Openness is policy, not contract: WMF says bots are ≥65% of its most expensive traffic and multimedia bandwidth grew 50% on AI scraping (Apr 2025 statement). Rate limits, UA enforcement, and pressure toward Enterprise can tighten without notice — the free paths have no SLA.
  • The donor funnel is the existential dependency: 91% of FY2024-25 revenue is donations (Verified Metrics), raised mostly from on-wiki banners. AI answer surfaces that consume the corpus without sending readers erode the thing that pays for the dumps an index relies on.
  • inferred Paid structured access is becoming the sanctioned bulk path for AI use. Basis: Enterprise revenue +148% to $8.3M with AI customers (Pleias, Nomic AI) named in the FY2024-25 Enterprise report, while the same fiscal year's crawler statement asks scrapers for "responsible reuse".
  • License compliance is per-object for media: text is uniformly CC BY-SA but each Commons file carries its own license, and machine-readability of legacy license metadata varies — bulk media reuse needs file-page resolution, not corpus-level assumptions.
  • Suppressed revisions and courtesy deletions (BLP, copyright, oversight) must propagate to the index; a dump-only pipeline keeps text the wiki has legally removed.
