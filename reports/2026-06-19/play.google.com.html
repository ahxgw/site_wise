Context

play.google.com is the storefront for Google Play: the default distribution channel for apps on 3B+ Android devices, plus books and audiobooks. Google monetizes it with a service fee on paid apps, in-app purchases, and subscriptions — historically 30%, now mostly 15% or less. For an index builder, the interesting object is not the page but the application ID (the reverse-DNS package name like com.whatsapp): globally unique, stable, printed in every listing URL (?id=…), and shared with the APK, the manifest, and every other Android tool. Unlike Amazon, Play does not hide its catalog: app detail pages are crawlable, carry microdata, and are enumerated in a public sitemap. The bigger story is legal — the Epic v. Google injunction now compels Google to share its app catalog with rival stores and allow third-party distribution and billing. inferred A sanctioned bulk catalog feed is arriving by mandate, not by API design. Basis: the Supreme Court let stand an injunction requiring Google to "share its catalog of apps with rivals," with catalog sharing due to "come into force next summer" (see Verified Metrics).

Retrieval Notes (site-specific deltas)

Only where Play differs from the default platform assumptions.

Identifier & dedup

The application ID (reverse-DNS package name, e.g. com.whatsapp) is the canonical app key: globally unique, stable, in every URL's id= param. Books carry separate Google Books volume IDs (id=Eoz0bGACG60C), bridging Play to the Books namespace. hl/gl params create per-locale listing variants — dedup on the ID, keep locale as a facet.

Retrieval unit

The app listing (per package), with offer (price/IAP), rating summary, and the data-safety/permissions block as sub-records. For Play's other corpora, the unit is the book or audiobook volume.

Cross-modal joins

Everything joins on the package ID: icon/screenshots (CDN), rating summary, developer entity, category. The package ID also joins outward to the APK and to security feeds — a strong cross-ecosystem key, unlike most platforms' internal-only IDs.

Authoritative content field

Developer-supplied titles are keyword-stuffed for store search; least trustworthy. Data-safety labels, declared permissions, and content rating are closer to ground truth; the rating summary carries quality signal but is an adversarial surface (review/install farms).

Freshness & recrawl

The sitemap batch is regenerated frequently (observed batch 2026-06-14, five days old). App metadata churns on each release; ratings append continuously; book catalog is near-static.

Quality & spam

Play-specific: cloned/repackaged apps with near-identical package names, keyword-stuffed titles, fake reviews and installs, and malware waves that trigger mass removals (peak ~3.6M apps fell toward ~1.85M). Icon/screenshot similarity is the strongest impersonation signal.

Internal vs external search

Unusual delta: Play feeds its own catalog to web crawlers (public sitemap, no AI-crawler block, full static HTML). The on-device Play search is the primary discovery surface, but Play pages are deliberately web-indexable — closer to Wikipedia's posture than Amazon's wall.

User query profile

App name ("whatsapp"), branded + platform ("free pdf scanner android"), and category browse. High install intent; little long-form text retrieval except in the Books corpus.

Indexing Plan

  • Retrieval unit: app listing keyed by package ID; offer, rating summary, and data-safety block as sub-records. Books/audiobooks keyed by Google Books volume ID.
  • Primary keys & joins: package ID (apps), volume ID (books); locale (hl/gl) as a facet; package ID joins to APK and security corpora.
  • Ingestion paths, ranked: 1) Public sitemap + crawl of /store/apps/details — no auth, microdata-rich, the rare open major-platform surface (Probe Log). 2) The Epic-mandated catalog-sharing feed (due summer 2026) — a legally compelled bulk path; watch for its spec. 3) Play Developer API — own-catalog only, useless for third-party indexing. 4) Review bodies — none; /store/getreviews is Disallowed, so design for ratings-only.
  • Recrawl cadence: top-app metadata + ratings daily; long-tail app metadata weekly (release cadence); books monthly; re-pull the sitemap index daily to catch the fresh batch.
  • Derivatives to generate: icon/screenshot embeddings for clone & impersonation detection; permission/data-safety normalization; title de-spam; package-ID ↔ APK ↔ developer concordance.
  • Demo to build: a sitemap-driven Play indexer — walk the 35,825 shards, fetch /store/apps/details, extract microdata (name, rating, ratingCount, price, category, image), emit JSONL, and report which fields need JS vs arrive in static HTML.

Risks to the Index

  • Catalog sharing reshapes the legitimate path: the injunction forces a bulk feed to rival stores by summer 2026 (SCOTUS denial) — potentially a sanctioned ingestion path, but format, terms, and eligibility are unpublished.
  • Regional fragmentation: post-Epic billing/distribution changes roll out by country (EEA/UK/US first, global Sep 30 2027 — dev blog), so the catalog/offer surface differs by region during the transition.
  • Open surface could close: the current single User-Agent: * robots group with no AI-crawler block is the exception among major platforms; Google could tighten it. The index's cheapest path is also its most revocable.
  • Review bodies are permanently closed: /store/getreviews is Disallowed and no API carries review text — plan for rating summaries only.
  • High deletion rate: mass policy-driven removals churn the catalog (peak ~3.6M → ~1.85M apps), so the index needs aggressive tombstoning, not just additions.

Inferences

  • A sanctioned bulk catalog feed is arriving by legal mandate rather than API design. Basis: the injunction's "share its catalog of apps with rivals" requirement upheld by SCOTUS (Oct 6 2025), force date summer 2026, with no equivalent consumer catalog API on Google's developer portal.
  • The application ID is the dedup/join key across the Android ecosystem. Basis: the reverse-DNS package name appears as the id= param in the listing URL (Probe Log) and is the same identifier used by the APK manifest and security feeds.
